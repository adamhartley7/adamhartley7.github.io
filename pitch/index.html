reading the graph

Two moments change everything.

For the first stretch, cost and value climb together, cost slightly ahead, you are paying to get the team up to speed. Then two things happen.

the first marker · upload

TOP-1 analyzes history and tests forecasting

The public analyzer explains past usage locally. Separately, TOP-1 is testing whether historical usage can support a useful low, likely, and high pre-run cost range. That forecast is not live in the public analyzer.

the second marker · implementation

TOP-2 tests whether the curve can bend

The research compares routing cost and quality under controlled benchmarks. TOP has not established a customer cost outcome.

the honest twist

The Jevons effect.

Jevons described a rebound effect in coal use: when an efficiency improvement lowers the cost of useful work, people may use more of it. Lower unit cost does not always produce an equally large fall in total spending.

AI may show a similar rebound effect. When each task gets cheaper, some teams may run more tasks. Total spend may therefore fall less than unit cost, stay similar, or rise. TOP has not yet measured which outcome is typical.

That makes a guaranteed bill-reduction pitch unsafe. The slider shows possible scenarios, not a forecast or customer result.

the risk

A lower unit price may lead to more usage, so a promised bill reduction may not appear in full. The size of that rebound has to be measured.

the opportunity

If lower unit cost increases AI use, forecasting and governance become more important. That is the opportunity TOP-1 is testing. TOP-2 remains a separate R&D track.

The useful business question is whether lower-cost work preserves quality and creates enough value to justify the spend. TOP-2 is an unshipped routing hypothesis. It needs controlled quality and cost tests before TOP can claim that it improves value per euro.
